Daniel Venton (Jan 28 2022 at 13:11):

We're really not talking about an individual here. A business is retrieving patient data in order to customize a quote (in theory) to the patient. No insurance company want's to disclose the confidential price structure they pay for services to competitors. So the fact of which company is "brokering" the exchange is important to the source payer. A patient access app where the data is being provided to a member (essentially directly), with little risk of it being used for competitive advantage is one thing. But providing bulk data to be mined for contract details to a competitor is different. This ties in with the other conversation of what data to expose in #implementers. In order to comply with laws and business objectives there will need to be a layer that takes into account: Person doing the request, The patient that is the subject, The reason for the request, The intermediary, Consent filings.

While payers are obligated to share clinical data with other payers, they are not obligated to share business confidential data. So if a payer is not obligated they will not do so. And I don't think "how much was paid for service" will ever be categorized as clinical. What this means is that each payer, individually, will come up with a method that "authorizes" financial data exchange. Likely limited to when the intermediary is not know to be a competitor.

Lloyd McKenzie (Jan 28 2022 at 15:54):

If the user is the patient, then I think by patient access, you need to give them everything - as they have the right to see everything, regardless of what tool they're using. If the user is someone from a payer, then you filter what they're allowed to see. You do that regardless of what scopes they ask for. There's always been a requirement to filter beyond what the scopes allow. The purpose of the scopes is to give the user control over what the system accesses. It doesn't allow the user to decide what their own access rights are.

Lloyd McKenzie (Jan 28 2022 at 17:24):

This isn't an IG requirement. This is a business requirement. If a business has rules about certain data not being allowed to be shared with certain recipients, they need to enforce that. You can't expect the requester to self-limit what they ask for.

Paul Church (Jan 28 2022 at 18:20):

Great! The business can have their auth server only return appropriate scopes for the requester.

Lloyd McKenzie (Jan 28 2022 at 18:52):

At best, scopes can define what resources to return. Scopes don't (at the moment) allow redaction of data from instances. And that's absolutely something that systems will need to be able to do. And there will be certain situations where scopes aren't sufficient either. If a requester provides a scope of "all lab results" and patient consent or organization policy says "lab results related to HIV testing and certain other sensitive tests can't be shared", then the server is obligated to do that additional filtering. And they can't count on the client to specify some fancy scope that says "all lab results excluding the ones you consider sensitive".

Daniel Venton (Jan 28 2022 at 19:17):

The component that creates the auth token will have to pass along all information needed for the "data limiter" to make a decision about the subset of data that will be returned. Right now the only scope defined by HL7 is the broad (has access to X resources) and I believe smart says (has access to patient y). Every other data point that the data limiter needs to make decisions will have to be placed in the auth token (or looked up with data in token). And this will be custom coding between the token generator and the data holder. Is it up to HL7 (smart) to say, "this is how in the token authorization is provided to get HIV type observations." I don't think so, there are too many rules for various jurisdictions to cover (unless it's a general scope rules engine).

Lloyd McKenzie (Jan 28 2022 at 19:34):

The auth token is not necessarily going to convey all the information needed by the data limiter - unless the auth server happens to be aware of all patient consents and all organization policies. Even then, that's not going to help with stripping data elements. Auth scopes don't have that granularity - and can't reasonably ever be expected to.
