Lloyd McKenzie (Jun 18 2019 at 00:06):

@Debi Willis There's two different types of 'joining'. No payment is required to participate. You can join calls, read specs and submit feedback without paying a dime (excluding internet connection fees :>). You can even come to work group meetings, though that requires paying quite a few dimes - and even more for not being a member. What you can't do is:
1. count as one of the 'initial members' that justifies formation of the work group
2. serve as a co-chair (or in any other organizational leadership capacity - e.g. board of directors)
3. formally 'vote' on a standard - i.e. provide feedback which determines whether a given specification will actually be approved as a standard or not
For #1, I'm pretty sure that when we start actively soliciting, #1 isn't going to be a problem. (That said, anyone on this thread, if you're willing to put your name on the list of initial members and you're an active member, please stick up your hand :>)

For #2, we could seek an exemption or see if we can get someone funded gratis - and there may well come a time where that will be desirable/appropriate. But it would be better to submit the request for a new work group without having to do that

For #3, I think we'll have to accept that, in most cases, patient feedback will come in as non-voting feedback. Such feedback still counts and is still considered, but isn't necessarily processed as quickly and can't "block" publication. Because HL7 is so strongly consensus-based, I expect this won't prove to be a significant issue in practice. If it turns into a problem, we'll tackle it then.

Lloyd McKenzie (Jun 18 2019 at 02:28):

HL7 has always been open to participation from non-members. However, FHIR's movement to gForge (soon? to be Jira) for both ballot and non-ballot comments has significantly boosted the ability for non-members to provide feedback (and ensure it gets processed). Work groups look at everything, but will typically address ballot-related feedback first - though priority is also driven by the significance of the feedback. The other difference is that if a work group disregards a comment of a negative voter, and the spec is going "normative" (i.e. locked down), the negative voter can ask all of the other voters to consider their comment and, if enough voters stand with them, they can prevent the spec from proceeding. Non-vote comments have no such power.
